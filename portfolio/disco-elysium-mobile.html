Client: ZA/UM
Role: Game Design • UI • UX
Platform: Android • iPhone
Disco Elysium Mobile is a mobile adaptation of award winning PC game Disco Elysium, an open world role playing game. You play as a detective solving both a murder, and the mystery of your own identity.

As a senior designer at ZA/UM, I was tasked with re-developing Disco Elysium for android and iOS. In this role, I led a team of 15 developers through the entirety of development, from concept to release.

The Design Challenge

The project began with figuring out the most suitable way to bring Disco Elysium to mobile, enabling those without PCs and consoles to experience the story. Disco Elysium is a massive game; with over 1M words of dialogue, full voice acting, as well as a soundtrack. A direct port to mobile would be impossible, as phones are far less performant and have far less storage than required. As well as this, phones, and the way people use them, are not conducive to the experience of playing a CRPG.

The goal became re-developing Disco Elysium from the ground up, using the assets we have to create a game more suitable for mobile play, and in doing so, giving a much wider audience the chance to engage with the story for the first time.

Defining the Concept

As one of the key goals is to deliver Disco Elysium to a wider audience, I created personas for our expanded target player profiles. In identifying player needs, I came to the conclusion that the most suitable format would be something more like an interactive graphic novel than an open world exploration game.

In terms of design changes, it was important to me that we stay true to Disco Elysium’s artistic vision, and keep it free of the kinds of dark patterns you'd expect to find on mobile games. I wanted to change the format as little as possible, and let the art and writing speak for themselves. We would offer the player a premium experience, with a free demo so they understood what were buying.

As for the visuals, I wanted to keep Disco Elysium's painterly aesthetic; expand it out of the portraits and into the world itself. All art would have a consistent fidelity, and fit the style of presentation. The film tape motif lent itself well to being thematically illustrative of a comic, so I wanted to lean into this aspect.

Redesigning the Format

Instead of an open world of tasks for the player to travel between, the game would be a collection of chapters and levels to pick from. Each level would contain all the relevant characters and locations to explore, and have the same task reframed as a goal. This meant that the player would still gets the freedom to pursue the most interesting thread, but there also are several benefits:

  • By cropping off-topic dialogue inside a level, there would be less narrative disruption, so that they player doesn’t get as lost in the lore.
  • Framing levels via their narrative goals makes progress towards any goal very clear, it’s easy for the player to decide what to do next, and easier to pick up after a break in play.
  • Splitting the game into a level structure allows us to deliver the game in 10 minute portions, with natural stopping points; very suitable for mobile play.

Breaking down all of Disco Elysium into chapters and levels was a massive challenge, and required always keeping in mind how all the narrative events needed to be sequenced.

Mobile first interactivity

Instead of using buttons or a mouse to select, the controls would be gesture based. The player would swipe up to select and inspect, and swipe sideways to change option or dismiss. Paired with the film tape aesthetic, we ensured the UI had a secondary motion and tilt sensitivity to make it feel like a physical medium. This worked really well for selecting dialogue options and trying skill checks, as it simulated the experience of being on a traditional film editing machine; the UI would slide, spin and latch with momentum.

As an extra treat, several “minigames” were developed to make use of a phone as the native controller - utilizing the gyroscope to look around 360 degree photospheres, or drawing on the whole touch screen.

To ensure that any player could use it, all of these mobile style UX patterns could be changed to simple button inputs in the options menu.
